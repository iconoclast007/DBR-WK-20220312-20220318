Well, those stones were for a “sign” (see 1 Cor. 1:22 and comments in that Commentary) and a “memorial” to future generations of Jews as to the miracle God performed by cutting off the waters of Jordan so that the Israelites could cross over on dry ground. Again, this is a great picture of the death, burial, and resurrection of Christ as is pictured in baptism. Death is pictured by a stone. We say about death, “he is stone cold dead on the market”; “he is still as a stone.” A grave is marked most often by a stone. When God drowned Pharaoh and all his troops at the Red Sea, Moses said, “they sank into the bottom AS A STONE” (Exod. 15:5), and “by the greatness of thine arm they shall be as still AS A STONE” (Exod. 15:16). In that last reference, it also says, “till thy people pass over, O LORD, till the people pass over, which thou hast purchased.” That’s what happened in Joshua 3–4 at the crossing of the Jordan. So a stone represents death. Look at Revelation 18:21. “And a mighty angel took up a STONE like a great millstone, and CAST IT INTO THE SEA, saying, Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all.” So going into that water is a picture of death and burial. Joshua hauls twelve stones out of that river bed and stacks them up in the Promised Land, and that’s a picture of resurrection. “Buried with him by baptism into death . . . raised . . . [to] walk in newness of life” (Rom. 6:4): that’s what happens to you at salvation. Of course, there is no interval between the deliverance and victory that were secured for the Christian at his Saviour’s death as there was between the Red Sea and the Jordan. But the Holy Spirit records these events in the Old Testament so you up here in the New Testament can understand the sequence of how the Lord works in your life. Those Israelites got saved at the Passover, they got immersed at the Red Sea and died to the Devil’s hold over them, they went through the wilderness journey and had to die to self, and then they crossed over Jordan into Canaan and got the victory. But if you want to see something interesting, look at Psalm 114:3. “The sea saw it, and fled [that’s the Red Sea; see Psa. 114:1]: Jordan was driven back.” There the Red Sea crossing and the Jordan River crossing are given right together with no interval of forty years in between. That typifies the Christian experience at salvation. Your salvation wasn’t a process, like those Old Testament saints; it was instantaneous. When you got saved, you didn’t just get your sins forgiven and get out of Hell, you got: \begin{compactenum}
\item Born again: you were given a new, live spirit.
\item Placed in Christ: you were joined to the Lord in one spirit.
\item Spiritually circumcised: your soul got cut loose from the body of sinful flesh so it could not be affected by it.
\item Sealed by the Holy Spirit: you are guaranteed to get to Heaven no matter what.
\end{compactenum} }
